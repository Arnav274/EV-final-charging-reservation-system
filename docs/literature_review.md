## EV Charging Management: Background

### 1. The Core Problem

The proliferation of battery electric vehicles has brought range anxiety — the driver's uncertainty about whether remaining charge is sufficient to reach a destination — to the forefront of urban transport research. A recent comprehensive review of technical and economic aspects of EV charging management identifies two interdependent scheduling sub-problems that any reservation platform must address: *where* to charge (station selection) and *when* to charge (slot allocation) [CITE: Energies 2023, doi:10.3390/en16093669]. Addressing only one sub-problem in isolation produces systems that are either spatially optimal but temporally naive, or vice versa, leaving unresolved congestion and suboptimal driver experience.

### 2. Distance-Based Station Selection

Shortest-path algorithms, and Dijkstra's algorithm in particular, constitute the dominant approach to distance-based station selection in the EV literature [CITE: Energies 2023, doi:10.3390/en16093669]. Dijkstra's algorithm finds the minimum-weight path from an origin node to all reachable destinations on a weighted graph, making it well suited to road-network topologies where edge weights represent travel distance or time. The present project implements Dijkstra on a complete graph whose nodes are candidate charging stations, consistent with the graph-based formulation reported in the literature. This implementation serves as a transparent, academically reproducible distance baseline. As a higher-fidelity alternative, the Open Source Routing Machine (OSRM) is integrated to supply road-network distances derived from OpenStreetMap, enabling direct empirical comparison between the pure graph-theoretic approach and a production-grade routing engine.

### 3. Queue-Based Station Selection

Predicting waiting time at multi-server service facilities is conventionally addressed through the M/M/c (Erlang-C) model, which assumes Poisson arrivals, exponentially distributed service times, and *c* identical parallel servers [CITE: Kleinrock, *Queueing Systems Vol. 1*, 1975]. Under the stability condition ρ < 1, Erlang-C yields a closed-form expression for the expected waiting time W_q. The present project extends the static Erlang-C formulation with a reservation-aware variant that adjusts both the effective arrival rate λ and the effective server count *c* in real time, incorporating the count of future reservations overlapping the user's predicted arrival window. This dynamic adjustment is consistent with the "enable reservations to reduce congestion" design principle articulated in the project supervisor brief [CITE: Zhang, project brief, 2024], and moves the model towards the predictive scheduling approaches identified as an open challenge in the wider literature.

### 4. Range Anxiety and Battery-Aware Routing

Range anxiety has been empirically confirmed as a significant barrier to EV adoption across European and North American markets [CITE: Weiss et al., *Transportation Research Part D*, 2024]. Battery state at the time of departure materially constrains the feasible set of reachable stations, yet many existing recommendation systems treat all stations within a geographic radius as equally accessible. The present project addresses this gap through a range-aware algorithm that incorporates the driver's current battery level and vehicle capacity, penalising any station whose projected energy consumption would leave fewer than 2 kWh of reserve on arrival — a safety buffer threshold consistent with the minimum-reserve conventions adopted in [CITE: Weiss et al., 2024].

### 5. Gap This Project Addresses

Existing EV charging management systems typically handle station selection or slot suggestion as independent problems and rarely expose both within a unified platform. The present work contributes three features absent from the reviewed systems: (1) real-time reservation-aware capacity reduction via live `current_occupancy` and predictive future-reservation lookahead, enabling more accurate congestion estimation than static Erlang-C; (2) combined where-and-when scheduling within a single REST API, linking station recommendation directly to slot suggestion; and (3) empirical algorithm comparison under controlled simulation, using one-way ANOVA and bootstrap confidence intervals to quantify performance differences across five strategies. The principal limitations of this evaluation are its reliance on synthetic arrival rates, simulation-based rather than field-collected reservation data, and the steady-state assumptions of the M/M/c model, which may not hold during transient peak-demand periods.
